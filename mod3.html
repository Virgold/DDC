Traffic Laws and Regulations

Traffic laws and regulations serve as the foundation for safe and orderly movement on our roads. They play a crucial role in protecting lives, preventing accidents, and ensuring a smooth flow of traffic. In this module, you will explore the importance of traffic laws and regulations, their key components, and the significance of adhering to them. Understanding and respecting these laws is essential for creating a safer and more harmonious driving environment for everyone.

Importance of Traffic Laws

Traffic laws are not arbitrary rules but a vital framework designed to ensure the safety of road users and the community as a whole. Enforcing these laws brings about numerous benefits that contribute to a safer and more efficient driving environment.

One of the primary advantages of traffic laws is the reduction of accidents and injuries. By establishing guidelines and standards for driving behavior, traffic laws promote responsible and safe practices. They address crucial aspects such as speed limits, signaling requirements, and right-of-way, which play a pivotal role in preventing collisions and safeguarding lives. Adhering to traffic laws significantly decreases the risk of accidents and the resulting injuries or fatalities.

In addition to enhancing safety, traffic laws facilitate the efficient flow of traffic. By clearly defining rights-of-way, signaling procedures, and rules for merging and overtaking, they promote a smooth traffic flow and prevent congestion. When drivers understand and follow these laws, traffic moves more smoothly, reducing delays and frustration for all road users.

Furthermore, traffic laws prioritize the protection of vulnerable road users, including pedestrians and cyclists. By establishing regulations that grant them rights and ensure their safety, traffic laws contribute to a more inclusive and equitable road environment. This helps create a culture of respect and consideration, fostering a safer space for all users to navigate.

Key Traffic Laws and Regulations

To maintain safety and order on the roads, it is crucial to familiarize yourself with key traffic laws and regulations. Below are some of these laws and regulations that every driver should be aware of.

Speed Limits

Speed limits are a crucial component of traffic laws and regulations that govern the maximum legal speed at which vehicles can travel on specific roads or in certain areas. These limits ensure the safety of drivers, passengers, pedestrians, and other road users.

By setting a maximum allowable speed, speed limits aim to reduce the risk of accidents, injuries, and fatalities on the road. When drivers adhere to speed limits, they have better control of their vehicles, increased reaction time, and improved ability to respond to unexpected situations.

Factors Influencing Speed Limits

Speed limits are determined based on several factors, including:

Road Design - the design and layout of the road, such as the number of lanes, presence of medians, curves, and intersections, which influence the appropriate speed limit.

Traffic Flow - the typical traffic flow on a particular road, where higher-volume roads with heavier traffic may have lower speed limits to maintain safety.

Road Conditions - the condition of the road surface, visibility, and any ongoing construction or maintenance work that may affect safety.

Pedestrian Activity - the foot traffic in certain areas such as school zones or residential districts which have typically lower speed limits to ensure the safety of vulnerable road users.

Environmental Factors - natural conditions like weather (e.g., rain, fog, or snow) that may require drivers to reduce their speed to maintain their control and visibility.

Types of Speed Limits

Speed limits can vary depending on the type of road and surrounding conditions. Common types of speed limits are discussed below.

  • Residential Areas
  • Urban Areas
  • Suburban and Rural Areas
  • Highways and Freeways

Speed limits in residential neighborhoods are typically lower, often ranging from 20 to 30 mph (32 to 48 km/h). These lower limits prioritize the safety of residents, children, and pedestrians in areas with higher foot traffic and potential hazards.

In urban areas, speed limits generally range from 25 to 45 mph (40 to 72 km/h), but they may vary depending on the type of street, traffic flow, and local ordinances. These limits account for higher traffic volumes, intersections, and potential congestion.

On suburban and rural roads, speed limits are typically higher, ranging from 45 to 65 mph (72 to 105 km/h). These limits take into account the road designs and the typically lower density of vehicles which may have fewer potential hazards.

Speed limits on highways and freeways are generally higher, often ranging from 55 to 75 mph (88 to 120 km/h), or even higher in certain regions. The Texas Transportation Commission can establish a maximum speed limit of 75 mph, 80 mph, or 85 mph on some highways if the highway is designed to accommodate that speed, and if a traffic or engineering study determines that it is safe and reasonable. These limits accommodate a faster flow of traffic for efficient long-distance travel.



Importance of Adhering to Speed Limits

Adhering to the required speed limit in a certain area is essential for several reasons.

  • Safety:Driving at a safe and appropriate speed reduces the risk of accidents, as it allows drivers to react to unexpected situations, maintain control of their vehicles, and stop within a reasonable distance.
  • Traffic Flow Adhering to speed limits helps maintain a smoother traffic flow, minimizing congestion, and reducing the likelihood of traffic incidents.
  • Legal ConsequencesViolating speed limits can result in traffic citations, fines, points on your driving record, increased insurance premiums, and potentially license suspension or revocation.
  • Public prescriptionRespecting speed limits demonstrates responsible and considerate driving behavior, fostering a positive public perception of drivers and promoting a safer road culture.

It is important to note that speed limits may change in different areas or under specific circumstances, such as construction zones, and school zones during certain hours, or when indicated by temporary signage. Being aware of and adhering to these changes is crucial for maintaining road safety and avoiding potential penalties.



Speeding Consequences and Risks

Speeding or exceeding the established speed limit in a certain area can have serious consequences and risks. Below are some of the key factors to consider.

Reduced Reaction Time: Driving at high speeds reduces the available time to react to hazards or unexpected situations on the road. A shorter reaction time increases the likelihood of collisions and decreases the chances of avoiding them.

Increased Stopping Distance:Higher speeds require longer distances to bring a vehicle to a complete stop. This extended stopping distance can lead to rear-end collisions, or the inability to stop in time to avoid hitting an obstacle.

Reduced Vehicle Control:Speeding compromises the stability and control of a vehicle. It becomes more difficult to steer, maneuver, and navigate curves safely. The risk of losing control and causing an accident significantly increases at higher speeds.

The magnitude of Impact: The force of impact in a collision increases exponentially with speed. Higher speeds result in more severe crashes and a greater risk of life-threatening injuries or fatalities.

Pedestrian Safety:Speeding in areas with pedestrians, such as residential zones or school areas, puts vulnerable road users at greater risk. Higher speeds reduce the time available to react and avoid collisions, posing a danger to pedestrians.


It is crucial to recognize and understand the potential consequences of speeding. By adhering to speed limits and driving at appropriate speeds, you can significantly reduce the risks associated with excessive speed and prioritize the safety of yourself and others on the road.

Safe and Responsible Driving

To ensure safe driving and respect for speed limits you should take note of the different positive behaviors.

Observe posted speed limits: Be aware of and adhere to the posted speed limits on different roads and in various situations.

Adjust speed to conditions: Adjust your speed based on road conditions, weather, traffic flow, and the presence of pedestrians or cyclists.

Plan Ahead: Plan your travel and allow sufficient time for your journey to avoid the temptation of speeding up due to time pressure.

Use cruise control: Utilize cruise control on highways to maintain a consistent and legal speed.

Be aware of surroundings: Stay alert to potential speed limit changes, school zones, construction zones, or other areas with specific speed restrictions

Lead by Example:Set a good example for other drivers by respecting speed limits and practicing safe driving habits.

Adhering to speed limits is not only a legal requirement but also your responsibility as a driver to ensure the safety of yourself, your passengers, and other road users. By driving at appropriate speeds, you contribute to a safer and more harmonious road environment. Remember, it's better to arrive a little later than to risk lives by speeding.

Traffic Signs and Signals

Traffic signs and signals are integral elements in maintaining order, conveying important information, and facilitating the safe movement of vehicles, pedestrians, and cyclists on the roads. They serve as visual communication tools that guide and regulate traffic flow. Understanding the meaning and significance of these signs and signals is essential for all road users. These are the things that you will learn in the next lessons.


Purpose of Traffic Signs and Signals

Traffic signs and signals serve several crucial purposes. Some of these are discussed below.

Providing Information: Signs convey essential information to drivers, pedestrians, and cyclists, such as road names, destinations, distances, and directions. They also indicate parking regulations, speed limits, and other important details.

Giving Instructions: Signs give instructions on how to navigate the road, including lane usage, merging, yielding, and turning movements.

Warning of Hazards: Signs alert road users to potential hazards ahead, such as sharp curves, pedestrian crossings, work zones, animal crossings, or traffic signals ahead.

Controlling Traffic: Traffic signals regulate the flow of traffic at intersections, ensuring a smooth and organized movement of vehicles while providing priority to different directions of travel.

Enhancing Safety: Signs and signals contribute to overall road safety by providing clear guidance, reducing confusion, and promoting uniformity in traffic movements.

Types of Traffic Signs

Traffic signs can be categorized into different types, each serving a specific purpose.


Regulatory Signs: These signs enforce traffic laws and regulations. They include speed limit signs, stop signs, yield signs, no-entry signs, one-way signs, and parking restriction signs. Regulatory signs must be obeyed by all road users.


Guide Signs: Guide signs provide information and direction to drivers. They include route markers, exit signs, street signs, and distance signs. These signs help drivers navigate and plan their routes effectively.

Informational Signs: Informational signs convey non-essential information such as upcoming services, attractions, or historical sites. They may include signs indicating nearby gas stations, rest areas, or tourist spots.

construction Signs: Construction signs are temporary signs placed during road construction or maintenance. They inform drivers of detours, lane closures, and work zone conditions. These signs ensure the safety of drivers and construction workers.

Warning Signs: Warning signs alert drivers to potential hazards or conditions that require caution. Examples include sharp curve signs, pedestrian crossing signs, animal crossing signs, slippery road signs, and construction zone signs. These signs prepare drivers for upcoming situations and prompt them to adjust their speed or driving behavior accordingly.



Traffic Signals

Traffic signals control the movement of vehicles and pedestrians at intersections.

Red Light: Requires all vehicles to stop completely behind the designated stop line or crosswalk.

Green Light: Indicates permission to proceed, but drivers should still yield to pedestrians and vehicles that are already in the intersection.

Yellow Light: Warns that the signal is about to change from green to red. Drivers should prepare to stop safely unless it is unsafe to do so.

Pedestrian Signals: Provides specific instructions for pedestrians, indicating when it is safe to cross the road.

Turn Signals: Indicates when it is safe to make specific turns, through arrows or separate signals.

Flashing Signals: Flashing red signals require drivers to come to a complete stop and proceed only when it is safe. Flashing yellow signals indicate caution and require drivers to move with care.


Importance of Adhering to Traffic Sign and signals

Adhering to traffic signs and signals is crucial for several reasons.

Safety: Following traffic signs and signals enhances road safety by reducing the risk of accidents, collisions, and conflicts between vehicles, pedestrians, and cyclists.

Orderly Traffic Flow Traffic signs and signals facilitate smooth traffic flow, preventing congestion and minimizing delays.

Uniformity and Predictability: Consistent adherence to traffic signs and signals by all road users creates a predictable and orderly road environment. When everyone follows the same rules, it reduces confusion and improves overall traffic efficiency.

Pedestrian and Cyclist Safety: Traffic signs and signals play a crucial role in ensuring the safety of pedestrians and cyclists by providing designated crossings, signaling the right of way, and alerting drivers to their presence.

Legal Compliance: Adhering to traffic signs and signals is a legal requirement. Failure to comply can result in traffic violations, fines, demerit points on your driving record, increased insurance premiums, and potentially license suspension or revocation.

Communication and Understanding: Traffic signs and signals serve as a universal language on the road, allowing drivers from different regions and backgrounds to understand and respond appropriately to the given instructions.


Understanding Traffic Signs and Signals

To effectively navigate the road and comply with traffic signs and signals you should take note of the guidelines below.

Familiarize Yourself: Take the time to learn and understand the meaning of different traffic signs, including regulatory, warning, guide, and informational signs. Study the colors, shapes, symbols, and text used on these signs.

Pay Attention: Stay alert and watch for traffic signs and signals as you drive. Be observant of changes in signage, especially in unfamiliar areas.

Follow Instructions: Obey the instructions given by traffic signs and signals promptly and accurately. Adhering to their guidance ensures your safety and the safety of others on the road.

Yield and Right of Way: Understand the right of way at intersections and yield to pedestrians and vehicles as indicated by signs and signals. Respect the flow of traffic and avoid unnecessary conflicts.

Stay Informed: Keep up to date with any changes or updates to traffic signs and signals in your area. Be aware of temporary signs and signals in construction zones or during special events.

Practice Defensive Driving: Even if you have the right of way, remain cautious and anticipate the actions of other drivers. Not all road users may follow traffic signs and signals correctly, so practicing defensive driving is essential to avoid potential hazards.

Remember, traffic signs and signals are in place to ensure the safety and efficiency of our roadways. Adhering to these signs and signals is a responsibility shared by all road users. By respecting and following them, you contribute to a safer and more harmonious transportation system.

Meaning & Common Rules of Right-of-Way

Right-of-way is a legal concept that determines who has the privilege to proceed first in specific traffic situations. It establishes a hierarchical order to ensure smooth and organized movement on the roads.

Importance of Understanding Right-of-Way

Safety: Right-of-way rules are designed to prevent collisions and promote the safety of all road users. By understanding and following these rules, drivers, and pedestrians can anticipate the actions of others and make informed decisions, reducing the risk of accidents.

Smooth Traffic Flow: Adhering to right-of-way rules helps maintain a smooth and efficient flow of traffic. When each road user knows when to yield and when to proceed, it minimizes conflicts and congestion at intersections and other areas where the right-of-way comes into play.

Courtesy and Respect: Respecting the right-of-way demonstrates courteous and respectful behavior towards other road users. It promotes a positive driving culture and helps create a safer and more pleasant environment for everyone.

Legal Compliance: Failure to yield the right-of-way when required can result in traffic violations, fines, points on your driving record, increased insurance premiums, and potentially license suspension or revocation.


Common Misunderstandings

Misunderstandings or misinterpretations of right-of-way rules can lead to confusion and dangerous situations. Below are some of the common right-of-way misconceptions.

Assuming Right-of-Way: Never assume other drivers will yield the right-of-way. Always be prepared to yield and proceed with caution.

Yielding at Stop Signs: Coming to a complete stop at a stop sign is necessary, even if you believe you have the right-of-way. Always yield to other vehicles as required.

Pedestrian Right-of-Way: Pedestrians have the right-of-way at crosswalks, but they should still exercise caution and ensure it is safe to cross before stepping into the roadway.

Turning Right on Red: In areas where right turns on red are permitted, drivers must yield to pedestrians and oncoming traffic before making the turn.

Emergency Vehicles: When emergency vehicles approach with lights and sirens, it is crucial to yield and provide them with a clear path to pass. You may pull over to the side of the road safely and allow them to proceed.

By respecting right-of-way rules, we contribute to safer roadways and create a more cooperative and efficient traffic environment. Remember, it is better to yield and wait for the appropriate time to proceed than to risk a collision or endanger others by asserting the right-of-way inappropriately. Stay vigilant, be courteous, and prioritize safety for yourself and others on the road.


Lane Usage

Traffic laws specify how lanes should be used to maintain order and safety on the roads. Drivers must follow these rules for changing lanes, merging, and keeping a safe distance from other vehicles. Lane usage refers to the proper and lawful positioning of vehicles within designated lanes on the roadway. Understanding and following lane usage guidelines is essential for maintaining order, promoting safety, and ensuring smooth traffic flow.
Let's delve into the topic of lane usage.


Lane Positioning

Proper lane positioning is essential for safe and effective driving. It involves being mindful of your lane boundaries and using the appropriate lane for your intended actions.

Some of the key considerations for effective lane positioning are discussed below.

Stay in Your Lane: Maintain your position within the boundaries of your designated lane. Avoid drifting into adjacent lanes or straddling lane markings.

Center Lane Usage: On roads with multiple lanes in each direction, the center lane is typically used for through traffic, while the outer lanes are used for merging, turning, or exiting. Stay in the appropriate lane based on your intended direction of travel.

Lane Courtesy: Practice lane courtesy by using the right lane for slower speeds and allowing faster-moving vehicles to pass on the left. Be aware of your surroundings and adjust your speed and lane position accordingly.

Lane Changes: When changing lanes, use your turn signals to indicate your intention to other drivers. Check your mirrors and blind spots to ensure it is safe to proceed. Yield to other vehicles and merge smoothly into the desired lane. Avoid making sudden or abrupt lane changes that can surprise or endanger other drivers.

Lane Discipline

Adhering to lane discipline contributes to safe and efficient traffic flow.

Keep to the Right: Unless passing or preparing for a left turn, drivers should generally keep to the right-hand lane. This allows faster-moving vehicles to pass on the left.

Passing on Multi-Lane Roads: When passing slower vehicles on multi-lane roads, use the left lane. After completing the pass, return to the right lane.

Slower Traffic: Slower-moving vehicles, such as trucks or vehicles towing trailers, should use the right-hand lanes to allow faster-moving traffic to pass safely.

Carpool Lanes and HOV Lanes: Carpool lanes or High Occupancy Vehicle (HOV) lanes are designated for vehicles carrying multiple occupants. Familiarize yourself with the specific rules and requirements for using these lanes.


Lane Usage for Turns

Proper lane usage is essential when making turns to ensure safety and efficiency. Below are some guidelines for different types of turns.

Right Turns: When making a right turn, approach the intersection from the right-hand lane, staying as close to the curb as possible. Yield to pedestrians and oncoming traffic, and signal your intention to turn. Maintain a smooth arc as you make the turn, keeping a safe distance from the curb or any obstacles.

Left Turns: When making a left turn, position your vehicle in the lane closest to the centerline or median. This allows for a clear view of oncoming traffic and provides sufficient space to complete the turn safely. Yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians, and use your turn signal to indicate your intention to turn. Wait for a safe gap in traffic before initiating the turn, and keep a close eye on approaching vehicles.

Multiple Turn Lanes: In some situations, intersections may have multiple turn lanes. Choose the appropriate lane based on your intended direction of travel and follow the lane markings and signs. If you are unsure, follow the arrows or signs indicating the specific lane for your desired turn. It is important to stay in your designated lane and avoid drifting into adjacent lanes during the turn.

Special Lane Usage Situations

In addition to standard lane usage, there are specific situations that require special attention and adherence to lane guidelines.

Bike Lanes: Respect designated bike lanes by refraining from driving or parking in them. Be aware of cyclists using the bike lanes and yield to them when necessary. Maintain a safe distance when passing cyclists and avoid any sudden lane changes that may jeopardize their safety.

Bus Lanes: Some roads have dedicated lanes exclusively for buses. Unless you are driving a bus or have explicit permission, refrain from using these lanes. Be aware of bus lanes and respect their purpose to ensure smooth and efficient transit for public transportation.

Construction Zones: Construction zones often have specific lane usage instructions due to road work or maintenance activities. Pay close attention to signage, signals, and instructions provided by road workers. Lanes may be shifted, narrowed, or temporarily closed, so follow the designated lanes and adjust your driving accordingly.
It is crucial to be aware of these special lane usage situations and follow the specific guidelines associated with them. Understanding and adhering to these guidelines contribute to maintaining orderly traffic flow, reducing the risk of accidents, and promoting a safer and more efficient driving environment.

By practicing proper lane usage in all situations, you play an important role in creating a smoother and more harmonious roadway experience for everyone.

Driving on Improved Shoulder

There are few instances where driving on an improved shoulder of the roadway is allowed. Under these circumstances, a driver may use the rightmost lane legally. To stop, stand, or park;

  1. To accelerate before entering the main traveled lane of traffic;
  2. To decelerate before making a right turn;
  3. To pass another vehicle that is slowing, stopped, disabled, or preparing to make a left turn on the main part of the roadway;
  4. To allow another vehicle traveling faster to pass;
  5. To follow directions given by an official traffic-control device; or
  6. To avoid a collision.

Drivers are required to use caution when using the improved shoulder and only in these situations. On the other hand, emergency vehicles and police patrols are not limited from using the shoulder lane. When you notice an emergency or police vehicle behind you on the rightmost lane, make sure to give space, slow down, and use caution.

Additional Traffic Laws and Considerations

In addition to the core traffic laws, specific regulations focus on critical aspects of road safety.

Seat belt laws require the use of seat belts by all vehicle occupants, including the driver and passengers. Seat belts are crucial for preventing serious injuries and fatalities in the event of a collision. Child restraint laws stipulate the use of appropriate child safety seats or restraints based on the age, weight, and height of children. These laws aim to protect young passengers and ensure their safety while traveling in a vehicle.

Impaired driving laws prohibit operating a vehicle under the influence of alcohol or drugs. Legal blood alcohol concentration (BAC) limits are established, and exceeding these limits is considered a criminal offense. Penalties for impaired driving can include fines, license suspension, mandatory alcohol education programs, community service, probation, and even imprisonment. These laws aim to prevent accidents, injuries, and fatalities caused by impaired driving, promoting the safety of all road users.

Distracted driving laws are in place to combat the dangers associated with using mobile devices while driving. These laws typically restrict or ban activities such as texting, calling, browsing the internet, or using handheld electronic devices while operating a vehicle. The focus is on minimizing distractions and maintaining full attention on the road. Violating these laws can result in fines, penalties, and potential legal consequences.

Traffic laws often include specific provisions and protections for vulnerable road users, such as pedestrians, cyclists, and motorcyclists. These laws emphasize the importance of sharing the road, respecting their rights, and providing them with adequate space. For example, drivers are required to yield to pedestrians in crosswalks, maintain a safe distance when passing cyclists, and be attentive to motorcyclists' presence on the road. These measures aim to reduce accidents and promote the safety of vulnerable road users who may be at higher risk due to their mode of transportation or limited protection.

Parking regulations govern where and how vehicles can be parked. These regulations include guidelines on parking in designated spaces, time restrictions, permit requirements, and prohibitions in certain areas. Following parking regulations ensures the smooth flow of traffic, accessibility, and safety for pedestrians and other vehicles.

Traffic laws may also cover requirements for vehicle equipment and maintenance. These regulations typically include rules regarding the proper functioning of lights, brakes, tires, and other essential components. Vehicles must meet certain safety standards to ensure their roadworthiness. Regular vehicle maintenance, inspections, and adherence to equipment requirements help prevent accidents and breakdowns.

School zone laws establish specific rules and speed limits in areas near schools to protect children and ensure their safety during drop-off and pick-up times. These laws typically require drivers to reduce their speed and exercise extra caution while passing through school zones. Violating school zone laws can result in fines and penalties due to the increased risk to vulnerable pedestrians.

Construction zone laws regulate driving behavior and speed limits in areas where road work or construction is taking place. These laws prioritize the safety of construction workers and drivers by mandating reduced speed limits, lane closures, and heightened awareness. Failing to comply with construction zone laws can lead to increased risks of accidents and penalties.

Traffic laws require drivers to use turn signals to indicate their intention to change lanes, merge, or make a turn. Signaling allows other drivers to anticipate and respond to these maneuvers, promoting smooth traffic flow and preventing collisions. Failure to use turn signals when required can result in traffic violations and compromise road safety.

Many jurisdictions have laws mandating vehicle insurance coverage to protect individuals involved in accidents. These laws require drivers to carry a minimum level of liability insurance, which provides compensation for damages and injuries caused by the insured driver. Compliance with insurance requirements helps ensure financial responsibility and facilitates the resolution of accidents and claims.

In Texas, the Health and Safety Code permits drivers to make an anatomical gift and donate an organ upon death. When a driver decides to authorize an anatomical gift, he becomes a donor in case of a fatal crash accident. In this case, all or some parts of the body organs will be donated for transplant, scientific study, research, therapy, or education.

Donors are, in a way, lifesavers who give hope to those in need of body organs. Those who wait for an organ donation often wait for weeks, months, or even years before getting a transplant.

Drivers who opt to become donors get an imprint on their driver’s license indicating that they authorize a donation in case of a fatal collision.

Litter Prevention

While litter may seem commonplace and negligible, in Texas it is a serious issue. In 2013, Texas Department of Transportation estimated that 1.5 billion pieces of litter left behind on Texas roads needed addressing. The year prior in 2012, the department spent $47 million just to clean up the roads.

Amazingly, 31% of these are cigarette butts that may even increase the risk of wildfires. Drivers caught littering in Texas roads may suffer the following consequences, depending on the weight of the trash:

  • Litter weighing up to 5 pounds – Class C misdemeanor with $ 500 fine
  • Weighing between 5 to 500 pounds – Class B misdemeanor with fine of up to $2,000 and / or as many as 180 days in jail
  • Weighing between 500 to 1,000 pounds – Class A misdemeanor with fine of up to $4,000

On top of these, community service may also be required for those caught in the act of littering.

Compulsary Insurance

Texas requires that all drivers insure their vehicles under the 30 / 60 / 25 rule of thumb coverage. This rule applies to the minimum insurance coverage that each driver must have in line with Texas laws.

  • $30,000 per injured person and minimum of $60,000 per accident
  • $25,000 insurance for property damage

While proof of insurance is adequate for drivers without violations, Texas requires SR-22 or Certificate of Financial Responsibility if it is court-ordered or required by the Department of Public Safety.
Drivers who were involved in a car crash are required to file their SR-22 certificate with the Department of Public Safety. Likewise, those who are cited twice for driving without insurance will be asked by Texas to file an SR-22.

Interaction with Law Enforcement

When asked by an officer to stop, promptly respond to give the officer time to carry out his task. Remain calm, follow instructions, and communicate honestly. Be ready to present your documents and identification. For a smooth interaction, these are do’s and don’ts when in a traffic stop with a police officer:

While being stopped by an officer, you should do the following:

  • Promptly reduce speed and pull over to the right side of the road as soon as it is safe to do so.
  • Park your vehicle on the right shoulder safely. If this is not possible, choose the safest area nearby with low traffic.
  • In conditions where there is low light or poor visibility, switch your hazard lights on.
  • For safety, an officer may opt to approach from the right side of your vehicle

Do the following while your vehicle remains parked:

  • Use your emergency brake and park mode, then switch off the engine.
  • The officer should be able to see your hands on the steering wheel, while the hands of passengers should be visible as well.
  • Lower your window—failure to do so is illegal.
  • Communicate openly with the officer. Before attempting to reach for documents or your license, notify the officer of where you will get them. If there is a firearm inside your vehicle, let the officer know. Your required documents should be located separately from the firearm.
  • Listen carefully and follow the officer’s instructions. When the officer approaches your vehicle, movements such as reaching for or searching for required documents, may be seen as performing something illegal.
  • Remain inside the vehicle unless you are instructed to exit the car.

These are what you should not do in your interaction with an officer:

  • Reach or search for documents of license even before or while the officer approaches your car
  • Attempt to leave a traffic stop while the officer is still carrying out his procedure.

If you suspect that the vehicle calling your attention is not a police officer, drive slowly towards a populated area and call 9-1-1. You may wait for the officer to be identified before ending the call.

Rear-facing seats (birth to 12 months) From birth to 12 months, rear-facing seats are appropriate to transport your kids in your car. Choose the ones with higher weight and height limits so you can use it for much longer for your child. Rear-facing seats are the only option for infants when carrying them in your car.
For as long as your kid remains comfortable and tethered appropriately, you can use a rear-facing seat until the age of 3. When necessary, upgrade to a forward-facing seat so your kid can remain comfortable when seated in the car.

Forward-facing seats (1 – 3 and 4 – 7 years old) Similar to rear-facing seats, choose the one with the highest weight and height limits. You may use forward-facing seats for your kid until the age of 7, provided that he or she is still within the prescribed limits.

Booster seat (4 – 7 and 8 – 12 years old) Booster seats are the last child restraint system your kid can occupy until he or she finally reaches the height of a young teen. Until your kid is taller than 4 feet and 9 inches, remaining on the booster seat is the safe option.

While there are several products in the market, make sure the one you are buying is approved by federal standards. Traveling with the peace of mind that your kids are safely restrained frees you up to focus on driving.

Leaving Children in the Car

A vehicle left under the intense sun is going to heat like an oven. If you have a child with you in the car, do not leave your child alone while your vehicle is parked. When the temperature heats up, the child can suffer hyperthermia or heat stroke that can permanently damage internal organs such as brain, liver, and kidney. In extreme cases, heat stroke may lead to death.

In Texas, leaving a child younger than 7 years old alone in the car, without a guardian of at least 14 years of age, is a crime.

It is an offense classified as a Class C misdemeanor and a crime punishable under the Texas Penal Code.

Human Trafficking Prevention (SB 1831)

According to a report in 2016, 300,000 Texans are victims of trafficking and around 79,000 of them are children. The government of Texas is serious in clamping down on this menace, encouraging civil society to work together with the state in bringing down these lawless elements and taking them to justice.

As a citizen of the state, you can contribute by knowing the red flags of human trafficking. The Attorney General of the state lists down some of the indicators of a victim of sex trafficking:

  • Person seems overly fearful, submissive, tense, or paranoid.
  • Person defers to another person before giving information.
  • Person has physical injuries or branding such as name tattoos on face or chest, tattoos about money and sex, or pimp phrases.
  • Wears clothing that is inappropriately sexual or inappropriate for weather.
  • Minor is unaccompanied at night or cannot explain who they are with and what they are doing.
  • They do not possess identification documents because they are held by another person.
  • Person works long or excessive hours or is always available “on demand.”
  • Overly sexual for age or situation.
  • The person has multiple phones or social media accounts.
  • Signs of unusual and unexplained wealth—new jewelry, shoes, phones without any known form of income.
  • Person lives in a “massage” business or is not free to come and go.

To report human trafficking, dial 911 in an emergency and alert the police. Providing information such as pictures, license plate numbers, detailed information, and observations are helpful.

You can also report via National Human Trafficking Hotline at 1-888-373-7888, or text “Help” or “Info” to 233722. You may also reach out in this email: humantrafficking@oag.texas.gov

Understanding Road Markings

Road markings can be confusing, not only for new drivers but also for many experienced ones. Let's take a moment to refresh our knowledge and remind ourselves of the meaning behind these road-line patterns.

  • Yellow Lines on the Road
  • White Lines on the Road

Yellow center road lines are used to separate opposite flows of vehicular traffic.

Single Yellow Solid Line Surprisingly, a single solid yellow center line on a two-lane road permits passing "with extra caution." However, be cautious of drivers attempting to pass when the line is solid, as not all exercise the necessary caution required for such maneuvers.

Double Yellow Solid Lines Double solid yellow lines indicate no passing in any direction. If a broken yellow line runs parallel to a solid one, traffic on the broken-line side can pass when safe, while traffic on the solid-line side is not allowed to pass.

Broken Yellow Lines A broken yellow center line on a two-way road indicates that passing is permitted when safe to do so.

Double Broken Yellow Lines Double broken yellow lines denote reversible lanes. Overhead lane-control lights (red/yellow/green) will guide drivers on which lanes they can use.

Diagonal Yellow Stripes Yellow-painted islands with diagonal yellow stripes instruct drivers to stay to the right of these stripes and not drive on or over them. They serve as imaginary boulevards.

Broken Yellow Lines with Outside Solid Lines Inside broken yellow lines accompanied by outside solid yellow lines designate a two-way oncoming left-turn lane. Opposing drivers share this lane for left turns. This lane should only be used for turning, not for advancing forward.

Broken White Line White center lines separate traffic moving in the same direction and are most noticeable on one-way streets.

Solid White Line A solid white line indicates that lane changes are not allowed. A solid white line at the side of the roadway prohibits drivers from crossing it to bypass someone making a left turn. Some municipalities have removed these lines to prevent bottlenecks on single-lane roads caused by drivers held up by left-turning vehicles.

Thick Solid White Line A thick solid white line at a stop sign indicates that drivers must stop before the line. Intermittent vertical thick white lines indicate crosswalk areas without stop signs or normal traffic lights. Drivers must stop when pedestrians intend to cross at these locations. Some modern alternating yellow lights are placed at these "zebra" style crosswalks to alert drivers to pedestrian traffic. Drivers can proceed once pedestrians have cleared the intersection; there's no need to wait until the lights stop flashing.

Parallel Horizontal White Lines Parallel horizontal white-lined crosswalks are found at traffic-light or stop-sign intersections. These crosswalks tend to be busier than "zebra" crosswalks due to the presence of larger groups of pedestrians in urban areas. Drivers are generally more cautious at parallel solid-lined crosswalks because of the predictable pedestrian activity. However, drivers should not become complacent and must remain alert when approaching any crosswalk.

Zebra Style Lines "Zebra" crossings often see more close calls compared to horizontally lined crosswalks where stopping at a sign or red traffic light is mandatory. Even if municipalities mistakenly paint "zebra" lines where solid crosswalk lines should be placed, drivers must stop at stop signs.

Diamond or Bicycle Symbol Many inner-city roads feature solid white lines separating a diamond or bicycle symbol from the regularly traveled portion of the road. These symbols indicate lanes reserved for specific types of traffic flow, typically in the same direction as regular traffic. Vehicle drivers are not permitted to drive in bicycle lanes except where dashed white lines are present, usually near intersections.
